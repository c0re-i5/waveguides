  THE SHALLOW WATER EQUATIONS — Why Depth Controls Everything
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Tsunamis, tides, storm surges, and harbor oscillations are all
  governed by the shallow water equations (SWE). These are the
  wave equation specialized for water where the wavelength is
  much longer than the depth.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  THE EQUATIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  For a fluid with surface elevation η(x,y,t) above mean depth h(x,y):

  MOMENTUM (Newton's 2nd law for fluids):

      ∂u/∂t = −g · ∂η/∂x       (x-velocity)
      ∂v/∂t = −g · ∂η/∂y       (y-velocity)

  CONTINUITY (conservation of mass):

      ∂η/∂t = −∂(H·u)/∂x − ∂(H·v)/∂y

  where H = h + η is the total water depth, g = 9.81 m/s².

  For small waves (η << h), this linearizes to:

      ∂²η/∂t² = g · ∇·(h · ∇η)

  If h is constant, this is just the wave equation:

      ∂²η/∂t² = (g·h) · ∇²η

  with wave speed c = √(g·h).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  WHY "SHALLOW"?
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  The shallow water approximation holds when:

      wavelength >> depth     (equivalently: λ/h > 20)

  A tsunami has λ ≈ 200 km and ocean depth ≈ 4 km → λ/h = 50. ✓
  A tsunami IS a shallow water wave, even in the deep ocean.

  This seems counterintuitive: the deep ocean is "shallow" for a
  tsunami because the wave is so enormously long. The wave "feels"
  the bottom everywhere.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  DEPTH CONTROLS SPEED
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

      c = √(g·h)

  This single formula explains almost everything:

  DEEP OCEAN (h = 4000 m):
      c = √(9.81 × 4000) = 198 m/s ≈ 713 km/h
      A tsunami crosses the Pacific in ~12 hours at jet speed.

  CONTINENTAL SHELF (h = 200 m):
      c = √(9.81 × 200) = 44 m/s ≈ 160 km/h
      The wave slows dramatically as it enters shallow water.

  COASTAL WATERS (h = 10 m):
      c = √(9.81 × 10) = 9.9 m/s ≈ 36 km/h
      Walking speed. But the energy hasn't gone anywhere...

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  SHOALING: WHERE THE DANGER COMES FROM
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Conservation of energy flux:

      E · c_g = constant along a wave ray

  where E ∝ A² (energy ∝ amplitude²) and c_g is the group velocity.

  As depth decreases: c_g decreases → A must INCREASE.

  A tsunami in the deep ocean is only ~0.5 m tall. But as it
  approaches shore and the depth drops from 4000m to 10m:

      A_coast / A_ocean ≈ (h_ocean / h_coast)^(1/4)
                        ≈ (4000 / 10)^(1/4)
                        ≈ 4.5×

  The 0.5m deep-ocean wave becomes a 2+ meter coastal wave.
  Add focusing effects from bathymetry and it can reach 10+ meters.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  BATHYMETRIC REFRACTION — THE OCEAN AS A LENS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Since wave speed depends on depth, waves REFRACT when the
  bottom topography changes — exactly like light refracting
  through glass (Snell's law).

      sin(θ₁)/c₁ = sin(θ₂)/c₂

  Where:
    θ₁, θ₂ = wave angles
    c₁, c₂ = wave speeds = √(g·h₁), √(g·h₂)

  This is the SAME Snell's law from optical waveguides!
  The ocean floor shape acts as a lens:

    • Submarine ridges FOCUS waves (like a convex lens)
    • Submarine valleys SPREAD waves (like a concave lens)
    • Coastline contours bend waves to arrive nearly parallel to shore
    • Harbors can trap and amplify waves (harbor resonance)

  This is also the SAME physics as repo 3's weighted wave
  propagation: the Eikonal equation |∇T| = 1/c(x,y) gives
  the arrival time T at every point, where c varies with depth.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  NUMERICAL SIMULATION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  To simulate a tsunami, we solve the SWE on a 2D domain:

  1. DISCRETIZE space into a grid (Δx, Δy)
  2. DISCRETIZE time into steps (Δt)
  3. Apply the CFL condition: Δt ≤ Δx / (√2 · c_max)
     where c_max = √(g · h_max)
  4. Evolve using finite differences (FDTD)
  5. Handle boundaries: coastlines (reflective), open ocean (absorbing)

  The grid resolution must satisfy the Nyquist criterion from repo 2:
  at least 2 points per wavelength (typically 10-20 for accuracy).

  The same FDTD method works for acoustic and optical waveguides —
  only the parameters change:

    ACOUSTIC:  c = 343 m/s (air), rigid walls
    OPTICAL:   c = c₀/n, index-guided boundaries
    FLUID:     c = √(g·h), depth-varying speed

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  REAL-WORLD TSUNAMI EVENTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  2004 Indian Ocean:
    Magnitude 9.1 earthquake, rupture length 1300 km
    Waves reached 30 m height at coast
    Propagated across entire Indian Ocean in ~7 hours
    ~230,000 casualties — led to Indian Ocean tsunami warning system

  2011 Tōhoku (Japan):
    Magnitude 9.0, seafloor displaced ~5 m vertically
    Waves reached 40.5 m at max runup
    Crossed Pacific in ~22 hours
    Triggered Fukushima nuclear disaster

  In both cases, the physics is exactly what the shallow water
  equations predict: fast in deep water, slow and tall near shore.
